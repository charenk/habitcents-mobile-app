P2-4b visual direction · 3 candidates, stacked left to right

Each column is a live, tappable prototype applying one direction to the same four screens: log form, Habits tab (v2 check-in card), habit detail (v2 long arc + 3-state calendar), onboarding success. Answer the check-in inside each frame to feel the skip moment. Yellow stickies carry pros and cons, green carries the tradeoff. Comparison matrix and recommendation on the far right. A "Final" column gets appended here once Charen picks.

A · Refined native

SF-weight hairline icons, depth via soft shadows on white, current tokens tightened. The quietest possible read.

Pros

  • Lowest build cost: no icon assets, no new dependencies, mostly a token and shadow sweep
  • Most native read; disappears into iOS the way ADA winners do
  • Ages well; nothing to regret in a year
  • Hairline icon system is one geometric grammar, easy to extend

Cons

  • Risks being forgettable; nothing ownable at the screenshot moment
  • The skip, the product's one emotional beat, renders as quietly as everything else
  • Differentiation carries entirely on copy and the Kept number

Tradeoff

Maximum trust and minimum cost, bought with zero memorability. A is the floor every other direction must beat, not the ceiling.

B · Dimensional accent

Rounded type, gradient icon tiles with inner highlight, borderless cards on deeper shadows, tinted hero panel.

Pros

  • Most distinctive and ownable; instantly recognizable in a screenshot
  • Warmth suits a money app that forgives slips
  • Tiles give category and habit rows a scannable anchor
  • Tinted hero makes Kept feel like a place, not a row

Cons

  • Gradient tiles flirt with the exact tropes the calibration warns about; restraint depends on discipline per icon
  • Needs a real icon asset system (custom art per category and habit): highest cost and ongoing maintenance
  • Rounded type + gradients can read "casual game" next to money numbers

Tradeoff

Memorability bought with asset cost and trope risk. Works only if the tile set gets real custom art; with placeholder glyphs it reads cheap.

C · Native + motion layer

A's visuals untouched. Motion and haptics concentrated on the two money moments: the log save and the skip.

Recommended

Pros

  • Distinctiveness lives exactly where the product promise lives: the skip visibly becomes money (ring, pulse, count-up, success haptic)
  • Everything else stays as still and native as A, so the moment stands out
  • No icon assets needed; motion is code, not art
  • Honors reduced motion by design

Cons

  • Motion quality is unforgiving: 20ms of jank reads worse than no motion
  • Static screenshots look identical to A; marketing needs video
  • Adds reanimated + haptics work to the build (medium cost)

Tradeoff

The reward is felt, not seen in stills. You pay in motion engineering polish instead of icon art, and the payoff only lands on device.

Recommendation: C

Recommended

C is A plus a motion layer, so picking C gets A for free. The product's whole thesis is that a skip is a felt win worth real money; C is the only direction that spends its distinctiveness budget on exactly that beat and nowhere else. That is also the honest reading of the ADA calibration: restrained surfaces, purposeful motion.

If more visual heat is wanted later: adopt B's tile treatment for category icons only, as an accent inside C, once real icon art exists (P0-4). Do not adopt B's gradients on buttons or heroes.

What the pick unblocks: 05 (unification pass written against the picked tokens), 03 (Leak Scan visuals), 04 (Coach Moments). Downstream specs are token-based and survive any of the three picks.
